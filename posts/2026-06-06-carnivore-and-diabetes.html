How the Carnivore Diet Affects Blood Sugar and Type 2 Diabetes

If you're diabetic or pre-diabetic, going zero-carb might sound insane. The results people actually see tell a different story.

June 6, 2026

Cutting out all carbohydrates sounds like the last thing a diabetic should do, right? We've been told for decades that diabetics need to eat carbs at every meal. Balanced meals, Whole grains. Carbs with every snack. The food pyramid was essentially a diabetic nightmare dressed up as health advice.

But here's the thing that's becoming hard to ignore. Thousands of people with type 2 diabetes have gone carnivore and seen their blood sugar normalize. Not just improve. Normalize. We're talking fasting glucose in the the 80s, HbA1c dropping from 8-9% down to 5%, and people coming off insulin and metformin entirely.

Let's look at how this works, what to expect, and how to do it safely if you're diabetic.

Why Carnivore Works for Blood Sugar Control

The mechanism is almost painfully simple. Type 2 diabetes is a disease of carbohydrate intolerance. Your cells have become resistant to insulin, so glucose builds up in your bloodstream instead of being stored or used for energy. The standard medical response is to give you more insulin or drugs that force your pancreas to produce more.

But what if you just stopped eating the thing your body can't handle?

On a carnivore diet, you're eating zero carbohydrates. No sugar, no starches, no fiber, no nothing that raises blood glucose from the gut. Your body still needs some glucose. Your brain and red blood cells run on it. But your liver makes all the glucose you need through a process called gluconeogenesis. And it does it at a slow, steady rate. No spikes. No crashes. Just a flat line all day.

This changes everything for a diabetic. Without incoming glucose, your insulin levels drop to their physiological floor. Your cells become more sensitive to whatever insulin you do produce. Your pancreas gets a break. And your blood sugar stops looking like a roller coaster.

Works great.

What the Numbers Actually Look Like

The most dramatic changes happen in the first few weeks. People who've been diabetic for years report seeing normal blood sugar readings for the first time in a long time. Here's the typical pattern:

Fasting glucose. Most type 2 diabetics start with fasting glucose in the 120-180 range or higher. Within days of cutting carbs, that number starts dropping. Many people hit the 80-95 range within 2-4 weeks. If you're on insulin or sulfonylureas, this drop can happen fast enough to cause hypoglycemia. More on that below.

HbA1c. This measures your average blood sugar over 2-3 months. The first HbA1c test after starting carnivore is where you see the real impact. Drops of 2-3 percentage points are common. Going from 8.5% to 5.6% in three months is not unusual. That's the difference between uncontrolled diabetes and normal glucose metabolism.

Post-meal spikes. On a standard diet, even "healthy" meals cause blood sugar spikes in diabetics. A bowl of oatmeal with fruit can push someone to 200+. On carnivore, a meal of steak and eggs barely moves the needle. You might see a 5-10 point bump from the protein conversion to glucose. Compared to the 50-100 point swings you're used to, that's basically flat.

Insulin levels. This is the one most doctors don't test but should. High fasting insulin is the hallmark of insulin resistance. On carnivore, fasting insulin drops dramatically. People often go from 15-25 uIU/mL down to 3-6. That's what metabolic health actually looks like.

Medication Adjustment Is Not Optional

This is the most important section of this entire article. If you are on diabetes medication, especially insulin or sulfonylureas, you must reduce your medication before starting carnivore. Not after you see low blood sugar. Before.

The standard advice is to reduce insulin by 50% on day one. Some people need even more. Your blood sugar can drop into the normal range within 24-48 hours of cutting carbs, and if you're still taking the same dose of insulin, you will have a hypoglycemic episode. That is dangerous.

Work with your doctor. If your doctor isn't supportive, find one who understands low-carb approaches. There are more of them now than there were five years ago. The Carnivore 101 guide has a section on talking to your doctor that might help frame the conversation.

Monitor your blood sugar frequently, especially in the first two weeks. Test before meals, one hour after, and before bed. If you see numbers below 70, you need to adjust your meds further. Carry glucose tabs or honey even if you never plan to use them. Better to have them and not need them.

Honestly, the medication issue is why some doctors are hesitant about recommending carnivore for diabetics. It's not that the diet doesn't work. It's that the diet works too well and patients don't adjust their meds fast enough. Don't be that person. Take it seriously.

Does This Mean Carnivore Cures Diabetes?

This is where we need to be precise with language. Many people who go carnivore end up with normal blood sugar off all medication. That looks like a cure. But the underlying insulin resistance may still be there. You're just not challenging it with carbohydrates anymore.

Think of it like a peanut allergy. If you avoid peanuts, you don't have reactions. If you eat peanuts, you do. That's not a cure - it's management through avoidance. Same with type 2 diabetes and carbs. The carnivore diet removes the trigger, so the disease goes into remission. But if you go back to eating sugar and processed carbs, your blood sugar problems will come back.

Some people do report that after months or years of strict carnivore, they can reintroduce certain carbs without blood sugar spikes. The theory is that extended periods of low insulin and low inflammation can reverse some of the cellular insulin resistance. That might be true for some people, but it's not guaranteed. Most long-term carnivore eaters with a history of diabetes just stay on the diet and enjoy their normal blood sugar.

What About Type 1 Diabetes?

Type 1 diabetes is different. It's an autoimmune condition where your pancreas produces little to no insulin. Carnivore won't cure that. But it can still help by providing more stable blood sugar with fewer swings, which means less guesswork with insulin dosing.

Many type 1 diabetics on carnivore report needing less insulin overall and having much fewer hypoglycemic episodes. The flat glucose curve makes it easier to match insulin doses. But type 1s still need exogenous insulin. The diet removes the variable of carb counting, which is a huge simplification, but it doesn't eliminate the need for insulin entirely.

If you're type 1, the same medication adjustment warnings apply even more strongly. Work with your endocrinologist and monitor obsessively in the beginning.

Electrolytes, Hydration, and the Diabetic Kidney

Diabetics need to be extra careful with electrolytes when starting carnivore. The initial drop in insulin causes your kidneys to dump sodium and water. This is the famous "whoosh" of water weight that happens in the first week. For most people, it's harmless. For diabetics with compromised kidney function, it needs more attention.

Salt your food generously. Consider a sugar-free electrolyte supplement. LMNT is popular in the carnivore community. The adaptation phase guide has a detailed electrolyte protocol that's worth following.

If you have diabetic nephropathy or any stage of chronic kidney disease, talk to your doctor before starting. High protein intake can increase the workload on your kidneys. For healthy kidneys, this isn't a problem. For already compromised kidneys, it can accelerate decline. Get your kidney function tested first.

What the Carnivore Community Reports

The forums are full of diabetes reversal stories. People posting their lab results with captions like "My doctor doesn't believe me." Before-and-after HbA1c screenshots. People who were on 100+ units of insulin now taking zero.

A typical story: someone diagnosed with type 2 diabetes 10 years ago. On metformin and increasing insulin doses. HbA1c creeping up despite "doing everything right" according to their doctor. They try carnivore as a last resort. Within a month, their fasting glucose is in the 80s. Their doctor cuts their insulin in half. Two months later, they're off insulin entirely. Three months later, metformin is discontinued. Six months later, their HbA1c is 5.2% and their doctor calls it "remission."

These stories are so common at this point that they're almost cliche. But they're real. The mechanism is straightforward. Remove the thing that causes high blood sugar, and blood sugar goes down. It's not magic. It's just that nobody told them they could stop eating carbs.

That said, not everyone gets complete remission. People who have had diabetes for 20+ years with significant beta cell damage may not regain full normal glucose regulation. But even partial improvement - needing less medication, having fewer spikes, feeling better - is a win.

How to Start Carnivore Safely as a Diabetic

Step 1: Talk to your doctor and get your current meds adjusted downward before you start. Reduce insulin by at least 50% on day one. Have a plan for hypoglycemia.

Step 2: Test your blood sugar 4-6 times per day for the first two weeks. This is non-negotiable. You need data to make good decisions about medication adjustment.

Step 3: Eat fatty cuts of meat. Ribeye, 70/30 ground beef, lamb shoulder, pork belly. Do not do lean meat and chicken breast. Fat is your fuel now. Skimping on fat will leave you tired and hungry and more likely to quit.

Step 4: Salt your food and supplement electrolytes. The Carnivore 101 guide has exact amounts. Don't guess. Get it right.

Step 5: Wait for adaptation before judging results. The first 2-4 weeks can be rough as your body shifts from burning glucose to burning fat. Your energy might dip. You might feel irritable. Your sleep might get weird. Push through it. By week 4, most people feel dramatically better.

Step 6: Follow up with your doctor at 1 month and 3 months for blood work. You'll almost certainly need further medication reductions. Don't skip these follow-ups.

The Bottom Line

Type 2 diabetes is a disease of carbohydrate intolerance. The carnivore diet removes carbohydrates. The result is normalized blood sugar for the vast majority of people who try it. Not everyone achieves complete remission, but almost everyone sees improvement.

The real barrier isn't whether it works. It's whether people can handle the social pressure, the adaptation phase, and the medication adjustment. If you can navigate those three things, the odds of fixing your blood sugar on carnivore are extremely high.

Honestly, the most dangerous thing about using carnivore for diabetes is doing it without medical supervision and crashing your blood sugar from unadjusted medication. Everything else is manageable. Do it right. Get your labs done. Work with a doctor. And enjoy not thinking about blood sugar for the first time in years.
