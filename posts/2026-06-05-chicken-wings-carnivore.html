Are Chicken Wings Underrated on Carnivore?

The carnivore community loves beef (obviously), but chicken wings might deserve more respect than they get.

June 5, 2026

A post on r/carnivroe caught my eye the other day. A user named SavageCabbage11 basically said: y'all are sleeping on chicken. And honestly, they have a point.

The argument goes like this. Chicken wings, with the skin on, have roughly a 1:1 fat-to-protein ratio. Fry them in butter and the fat content goes even higher. They're cheap, they're easy to cook, and they taste great. So why does the community treat chicken like it's a dirty secret while happily eating dairy, fish, pork, and eggs?

It's a fair question. Let's break it down.

The Fat Argument Against Chicken

The main reason carnivores avoid chicken is the fat content. Chicken breast is basically protein powder with texture. Even chicken thighs with skin are leaner than most cuts of beef or pork. On a diet where fat is supposed to be 70-80% of your calories, eating lean poultry seems counterproductive.

But chicken wings are different. A whole chicken wing includes the drumette, the flat, and the tip. The skin-to-meat ratio is way higher than any other chicken cut. That skin is where the fat lives. When you cook wings properly - fried, baked, or air-fried in butter or tallow - you end up with a food that's actually quite fatty.

Are they as fatty as a ribeye? No. But they're not supposed to be. They're a different tool in the toolbox. And for people who can't afford ribeye every day, or who want variety, they work.

The Double Standard

This is the part that got me thinking. The OP pointed out something true: a lot of carnivores who won't touch chicken are perfectly fine eating dairy, fish, and pork. Let's look at the numbers.

Pork chops are leaner than chicken wings. Pork tenderloin is one of the leanest meats you can buy. Yet pork is widely accepted in the community. Bacon is practically a mascot food. Fish like cod and tilapia are extremely lean, but nobody argues they don't belong. Cheese has almost no fat compared to meat cuts when you factor in protein content. But dairy is fine.

So what makes chicken the exception? The omega-6 argument comes up a lot. Chicken fat is higher in omega-6 polyunsaturated fats than beef or lamb fat. That's true. But pork fat is also high in omega-6. Farmed fish can be even worse depending on what they're fed. And nobody's banning those.

It seems like the chicken aversion is more about dogma than data. Somewhere along the way, "red meat only" became the gold standard for some people, and anything else got labeled as suboptimal. Which is fine if that's your personal choice. But it's not a rule that applies to everyone.

Who Should Consider Chicken Wings

Chicken wings make the most sense for three groups of people.

Budget eaters. Beef prices keep going up. Chicken wings are still relatively cheap, especially if you buy them in bulk or frozen. A bag of frozen wings costs a fraction of what you'd pay for the same weight in beef. When money is tight, wings keep you carnivore without breaking the bank.

Variety seekers. Eating the same thing every day gets old. Even if you love steak, having a wing night once or twice a week breaks the monotony. Different textures, different cooking methods, different flavors. Keeps the diet sustainable.

People who react to beef. Some people genuinely don't do well with beef. Histamine intolerance, digestion issues, or just personal preference. Chicken is a completely different protein source and can work when beef doesn't.

How to Make Chicken Wings Work on Carnivore

The trick is fat. You need to add it. Don't eat plain baked wings and expect to feel great. Here's what works.

Cook them in butter or tallow. The OP mentioned frying wings in butter. This is the move. The fat from the cooking medium coats the wings and adds significant calories. An air fryer works great too - toss the wings in melted butter or tallow before cooking, then again after.

Eat the skin. This is non-negotiable. The skin is where almost all the fat is. If you're removing the skin, you might as well be eating chicken breast. Crispy, well-rendered skin is one of the best textures in cooking. Don't waste it.

Pair with other fats. Have some cheese on the side. Dip wings in butter. Add a side of eggs fried in tallow. The wings themselves get you partway there, but you still need to think about overall fat intake for the meal.

Watch the seasoning. A lot of pre-seasoned wing packets and hot sauces have sugar, maltodextrin, or other non-carnivore ingredients. Plain wings with salt is the safest bet. If you want heat, find a hot sauce with no added sugar - Frank's Red Hot Original is a common choice that works.

A good instant-read thermometer helps nail the cook on wings just like it does for steak. The ThermoPro TP20 is what I use and it handles wing temps just fine.

The Bottom Line on Chicken

Chicken wings are a legitimate carnivore food. They're not a replacement for beef, and they shouldn't be your only meat source. But as part of a varied carnivore diet, they work well. The fat ratio is better than people assume, the price is right, and they add variety that helps you stick with the diet long-term.

If the carnivore community can accept bacon, eggs, cheese, salmon, pork belly, and heavy cream - all of which have their own tradeoffs - then chicken wings deserve a seat at the table too.
