How Carnivore Heals IBS, SIBO, and Leaky Gut

Gut issues are complicated. But the mechanism behind why carnivore works is surprsingly simple once you understand what's actually happening.

June 5, 2026

If you've been dealing with chronic gut issues for years - IBS that flares up no matter what you eat, SIBO that keeps coming back after treatment, or that constant low-grade bloating and discomfort - you've probably tried everything. Low-FODMAP. Probiotics, Elimination diets. Antacids. Maybe even the antibiotic rounds for SIBO.

And maybe nothing stuck.

The carnivore diet takes a different approach. Instead of trying to manage which plant foods you eat, it removes all plant foods entirely. For a lot of people with stubborn gut problems, that's what finally works. Not because plants are inherently bad, but because the elimination mechanic is the most powerful tool in gut healing and carnivore is the purest form of it.

The Three Layers of Gut Issues

When people talk about gut healing on the carnivore diet, they're usually dealing with one or more of these three interconnected problems:

IBS (Irritable Bowel Syndrome). This isn't one thing. It's a collection of symptoms - cramping, bloating, diarrhea, constipation - without a clear structural cause. There's IBS-D (diarrhea-dominant), IBS-C (constipation-dominant), and IBS-M (mixed). What they all share is that something in your diet is triggering a dysfunctional gut response. For most people, that trigger is a fermentable carbohydrate or a plant compound that irritates the intestinal lining.

SIBO (Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth). This is when bacteria from the large intestine migrate up into the small intestine where they don't belong. Those bacteria feed on carbohydrates. When you eat anything with fermentable fiber, sugar, or resistant starch, the bacteria feast and produce gas, bloating, and inflammation. Standard treatments include antibiotics (rifaximin) or herbal antimicrobials, but recurrence rates are high because the underlying motility and diet issues haven't been addressed.

Leaky Gut (Intestinal Hyperpermeability). Your gut lining is a single layer of cells held together by tight junctions. When those junctions get damaged - from chronic inflammation, food sensitivities, stress, or alcohol - larger molecules can pass through into your bloodstream. Your immune system sees these molecules as threats and mounts an inflammatory response. This is the foundation for a lot of autoimmune conditions, skin problems, and systemic inflammation.

These three conditions feed each other. SIBO creates inflammation that worsens leaky gut. Leaky gut allows bacterial fragments and undigested food particles through, creating more immune activation. IBS is often the resulting symptom picture. Break any one of these cycles and the others start to improve.

Why Carnivore Attacks All Three

The beauty of the carnivore diet for gut issues is that it hits all three problems simultaneously with one intervention: removing plant foods.

For SIBO: The bacteria in your small intestine need fermentable carbohydrates to survive. No carbs, no fuel, no overgrowth. Protein and fat are absorbed high in the digestive tract - they never reach the bacteria in the small intestine in any meaningful amount. Within days of removing plant foods, SIBO symptoms like bloating and gas often decrease dramatically. It's like turning off the bacteria's food supply. For a detailed breakdown of how this works for SIBO specifically, check out the SIBO and IBS-C community report.

For IBS: Most IBS triggers are plant-based. FODMAPs, lectins, oxalates, and phytates all cause issues for people with sensitive guts. By removing all of them at once, you give your gut a clean slate. No guessing games about which specific food caused your flare-up. The variability in symptoms smooths out because the dietary input is completely consistent. For IBS-D, the lack of fermentable fiber means less water pulled into the colon and more formed stools. For IBS-C, many people find that without plant bulk irritating the colon, regular bowel movements actually improve. It sounds backwards, but the community reports it constantly.

For Leaky Gut: Plant compounds like lectins (especially in grains and legumes) and saponins can directly damage the tight junctions between your gut cells. Remove them and your gut lining gets a chance to repair. The amino acids from meat - particularly glycine, proline, and glutamine - provide the raw materials for rebuilding that lining. Bone broth is especially rich in these. And since you're no longer triggering an immune response from food antigens crossing your gut barrier, the systemic inflammation starts to calm down.

What the Adaptation Period Looks Like

Here's the part nobody likes to talk about: the first few weeks can be rough, especially if you're coming from a high-fiber diet.

Your gut microbiome is used to fermenting plant material. When you remove that fuel source, the bacterial populations shift dramatically. Some people experience temporary constipation as their gut adjusts to the lower stool bulk. Others get loose stools as their digestion works through the transition. Both are normal and usually resolve within 2-4 weeks.

If you have SIBO, you might notice die-off symptoms during the first week. When the bacteria in your small intestine start starving, they release endotoxins as they die. This can cause temporary worsening of brain fog, fatigue, and bloating. It usually passes within a few days. Drinking more water and staying on top of electrolytes helps.

The key is not to panic and quit. Give it at least 30 days of strict meat, salt, and water before judging whether it's working. For deeper gut healing, 90 days is a better target. Some people with severe SIBO or autoimmune involvement need 6-12 months. But most people notice meaningful improvement well before that.

The Lion Diet: The Gut Healing Protocol

If you want the most aggressive gut healing approach, the lion diet is worth knowing about. It's a subset of carnivore: only beef, salt, and water. Nothing else. No pork, no chicken, no eggs, no dairy, no spices, no coffee.

The idea is to reduce potential triggers to the absolute minimum. Beef is the least allergenic food for most people. Some people react to eggs (egg whites are a common allergen) or to dairy (casein and lactose). By stripping everything away except beef and salt, you give your gut the cleanest possible environment to heal in.

Most people don't need to go this extreme. But if you've tried standard carnivore and still have symptoms, the lion diet is the next logical step. Do it for 2-4 weeks, then slowly reintroduce other animal foods one at a time - eggs first, then dairy, then pork, then poultry. Pay attention to how each one affects your symptoms.

This is essentially the same protocol as the carnivore adaptation phase guide, just taken to its logical extreme.

Salt, Fat, and Water: The Forgotten Gut Healing Factors

Gut healing isn't just about what you remove. It's also about what you provide in abundance.

Salt. Salt is critical for digestion. Stomach acid production requires chloride. Low salt intake leads to low stomach acid, which means food sits in your stomach longer, ferments, and feeds SIBO bacteria. On carnivore, your sodium requirements increase because you're excreting more electrolytes through urine (lower insulin levels cause the kidneys to dump sodium). Most people need 5-10 grams of salt per day on carnivore. Don't be shy with it. If you're getting cramps, fatigue, or heart palpitations, you're almost certainly not getting enough.

Fat. Fat triggers bile release from your gallbladder. Bile is antimicrobial - it helps keep the small intestine clean. It also lubricates the digestive tract and helps prevent constipation. On a low-fat carnivore diet (lean meat only), you'll likely feel worse, not better. Aim for 70-80% of calories from fat. If you're struggling with diarrhea, increasing fat and decreasing protein often helps. If you're constipated, more fat and salt water usually does the trick.

Water. Your gut needs water to function properly. On carnivore, your water needs change - you'll need more than you did on a standard diet because you're excreting more electrolytes and processing more protein. Aim for at least 2-3 liters daily. Don't chug it all at once though. Spreading it throughout the day is better for digestion.

Reintroduction: The Part Most People Rush

Once your gut has healed - and you'll know because your digestion is consistent, your energy is stable, and your symptoms are gone - you might want to reintroduce some foods. This is where most people mess up.

The rule is simple: introduce one new food at a time, eat it for three days straight, and pay close attention. If you react, you know that food is a problem for you. If you don't react, you can keep it in your rotation, but don't add another food until you're sure.

Some people can reintroduce dairy after gut healing. Others can handle certain low-toxin vegetables. A lot of people discover they can't ever go back to grains or legumes without symptoms returning. That's valuable information. It doesn't mean you failed at carnivore - it means you learned exactly what your gut can and can't handle.

If you try to add everything back at once, you'll never know what caused the flare-up. And the flare-up might be severe enough that it takes weeks to recover, which is demoralizing. Slow reintroduction is worth the patience.

Practical Tips That Actually Make a Difference

Eat slowly. Gut healing doesn't happen when you inhale your food in five minutes. Give your digestive system time to process what you're eating. Chew thoroughly - mechanical breakdown matters more than most people realize, especially when you're not getting help from plant enzymes.

Consider bone broth. Simmer beef bones with salt for 12-24 hours. The collagen, gelatin, glycine, and glutamine in the broth directly support gut lining repair. It's not essential, but it accelerates healing for most people. Drink a cup daily during the first month.

Don't drink water with meals. This is a counterintuitive one but it matters. Drinking large volumes of water with food dilutes your stomach acid and digestive enzymes. If you have low stomach acid (common with SIBO and chronic gut issues), drinking during meals makes it even harder to digest food properly. Try keeping fluids to 30 minutes before or after meals instead.

Pay attention to temperature. Very cold foods and drinks can shock a sensitive digestive system and trigger cramping. Room temperature water and warm food are easier on a healing gut. Hot bone broth before meals can actually prep the digestive tract for incoming food.

The Timeline Nobody Wants to Hear

Gut healing on carnivore follows a pattern, but the timeline varies wildly depending on how damaged your gut is, how long you've had issues, and whether there are underlying structural problems.

First week: The initial shift. Bloating usually drops fast. Bowel habits may go either direction. Energy might dip during the transition.

First month: The settling period. Most people see major symptom improvement within 30 days. Digestive patterns stabilize. The frequency and consistency of bowel movements becomes predictable.

Three months: The healing phase. Gut lining repair is happening. If you had leaky gut, this is when you might notice improvements in symptoms that seemed unrelated - skin clearing, joint pain reduction, better mood.

Six to twelve months: Deep healing. For people with autoimmune involvement or severe SIBO, lasting change takes time. The immune system needs months of not being triggered to calm down fully.

The people who stick with it for a year report the most profound changes. But even the first 30 days gives you useful information about whether this approach works for your particular gut.

When Carnivore Isnt Enough

Honestly, carnivore fixes a lot of gut problems. But it's not a magic bullet. Some people need additional interventions.

If you've been strict carnivore for 90 days and your gut issues haven't improved, consider seeing a functional medicine practitioner. You might have underlying issues like low stomach acid that needs supplementation, a motility disorder that requires prokinetic support, or an infection that needs targeted treatment.

Carnivore creates the conditions for healing. It removes the insults and provides the raw materials. But if the underlying mechanism is broken enough, you might need more than diet alone. That's not a failure of the diet. It's just reality.

The good news is that most people with chronic gut issues find significant relief within weeks of starting carnivore. The mechanism is sound, the community evidence is overwhelming, and the risk of trying it for 30 days is basically zero. If your gut has been running your life for years, it's worth a shot.
