The First 3 Days of Carnivore Are Emotionally Brutal - Here's Why That's Normal

A fit 32-year-old woman started the carnivore diet to fix her SIBO. She didn't expect the hardest part to be saying goodbye to her chocolate and mochaccino rituals.

June 17, 2026

A post on r/carnivore this week stopped me in my tracks. A 32-year-old woman, fit and health-conscious, decided to start carnivore to deal with the SIBO she'd been struggling with for months, She's only three days in. And she's not struggling with hunger or cravings for bread or sugar in the way you'd expect.

She's struggling with the loss of her rituals.

"I always managed to get away with snacking on quality chocolate throughout the day and a treat before bed at night," she wrote. "I also loved Moccachinos. I would joke about my chocolate addiction to people." Day 3 and she's feeling "lots of emotional withdrawals to food." Her morning cup of coffee with chocolate is gone. The evening treat she looked forward to all day is gone. And it hit her harder than she expected.

This is the side of starting carnivore nobody talks about enough. So let's talk about it.

The Emotional Side of Starting Carnivore

When people write beginner guides for the carnivore diet, they focus on the physical transition. Electrolytes, fat adaptation, keto flu, oxalate dumping. All important stuff. But almost nobody warns you about the emotional withdrawal that comes with giving up the foods that were woven into your daily life.

For this woman, it wasn't about being hungry. She was eating enough meat. It was about the fact that her morning coffee ritual was gone. The chocolate she'd reach for in the afternoon. The treat she'd have while winding down at night. These weren't just foods. They were comfort. They were breaks in her day. They were habits she'd built over years, and removing them left a hole that meat and eggs didn't fill.

That hole is real. And it's normal.

Why Food Rituals Hit So Hard

Food is emotional. That's not a flaw, it's how humans work. We associate certain foods with comfort, reward, and connection. A mochaccino isn't just a drink - it's the signal that your day has started. A piece of chocolate at night isn't just sugar - it's the ritual that tells your brain it's time to relax.

When you take those away on day one of carnivore, your brain doesn't just miss the taste. It misses the emotional cue. The routine. The sense of normalcy. And because carnivore is such a hard restriction - no plants at all - there aren't easy substitutes. You can't swap in a "healthy" version of your mochaccino. You just have to sit with the absence.

That's legitimately hard. Harder than most people expect.

What Helps (Besides Just Toughing It Out)

The good news is that this phase passes. Most people report that within 1-2 weeks, the emotional cravings fade. Your brain rewires. You find new rituals. But there are things you can do to make the first few days less brutal.

Name what you're actually missing. Are you craving the taste of chocolate, or the break in your afternoon? Are you missing coffee, or the five minutes of quiet you spent drinking it? Once you separate the food from the feeling, you can replace the feeling with something else. A walk. A glass of sparkling water. Five minutes of stretching.

Build new rituals immediately. Your brain needs something to anchor your day around. Replace the morning coffee with a hot mug of bone broth. Replace the evening chocolate with a square of very fatty meat or a cup of hot tea (if you tolerate it). The specific replacement matters less than the fact that you have something to look forward to.

Give yourself permission to be sad about it. This one matters. A lot of carnivore content is overly positive - all about the benefits, the energy, the healing. And those benefits are real. But in the first week, it's okay to admit that this sucks. You're giving up things that brought you comfort. That deserves acknowledgment, not toxic positivity.

Upgrade your meat game. The first few days are not the time to be eating bland chicken breast. Eat the fattiest, most satisfying meat you can afford. A good ribeye cooked in butter with crispy salt. Bone marrow if you can find it. Slow-braised short ribs. The more satisfying your meals, the less your brain will look for satisfaction elsewhere.

This is where having a good cast iron pan pays off - a perfect sear on a fatty steak satisfies something deeper than just hunger. And a reliable meat thermometer means you nail the cook every time instead of ruining an expensive piece of meat and feeling even worse.

What This Woman Got Right

The OP in this thread did something smart. She posted on day 3, while she was in the thick of it. She didn't wait until she had a success story to share. She shared the messy middle. The part where she's eating enough meat but still feels like something's missing. The part where she knows intellectually that this is good for her gut but emotionally still wants her comfort foods.

That honesty is valuable. It helps other people who are on day 2 or day 4 and wondering why they feel worse emotionally instead of better. It normalizes the struggle. And it reminds us all that the emotional adjustment to carnivore is just as real as the physical one.

She's treating her SIBO by removing the plant fibers and carbohydrates that feed the bacteria. That's the right call. The original post on r/carnivore got a lot of supportive responses, and for good reason. Everyone who's been through those first few days recognizes the struggle. The people who say "just push through" mean well, but what newcomers actually need is someone to say "yeah, this part is hard, and it will pass."

The Bottom Line

The first few days of carnivore can be emotionally harder than you expect. That doesn't mean you're doing something wrong. It means you're human. Food rituals are real, and losing them hurts. But the hurt is temporary.

Within a week or two, most people stop craving their old comfort foods entirely. The mochaccino loses its pull. The chocolate doesn't call your name anymore. Your brain adapts, builds new pathways, and eventually the meat itself becomes the thing you look forward to. But getting there requires getting through those first days first.

If you're on day 2 or day 3 right now and feeling emotional about what you gave up - give yourself some credit. You're doing the hard part. It gets easier. Way easier than you think.

Just don't try to do it on bland food. Eat the good stuff. You deserve it.
